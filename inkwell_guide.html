Aislin's Inkwell
Your private writing studio
This is a tool built specifically for you and the stories you want to tell. It lives in a single file on your computer — no account, no login, no internet needed. This guide walks you through everything it can do.
Tap the arrow to begin →
Getting Started
Your Project Library

When you open the file, you land in the project library. Every story you've started lives here as a card showing its name, how many characters and worlds are in it, and when you last worked on it.

Click any card to open that story. Click + New Project to start something new — just type a name and press Enter.

Each project is completely separate. Characters in one story have no idea the others exist.

💡 A "← All Projects" link lives at the top of the sidebar whenever you're inside a project, so you can always get back here.
Section 1
Characters

Every character in your story gets their own card. Click the name to open it. Inside you'll find fields that go from specific to open:

Name, age, role → physical description, personality → backstory → relationships → a scratchpad with no rules at all.

Every field saves automatically the moment you click away. You'll see a quiet "Saved" in the sidebar. You never touch a save button.

💡 The scratchpad at the bottom is for half-formed thoughts. "Why does she cut her own hair? Figure this out." That kind of thing.
Characters — Detail
The Importance Slider

At the top of every character card is a 1–5 importance slider. Drag it left or right. The stars update in real time.

The character list always sorts highest-to-lowest — so your protagonist is always at the top, your ten background characters sink to the bottom.

The labels: ★ Extra → ★★ Supporting → ★★★ Secondary → ★★★★ Main cast → ★★★★★ Protagonist

💡 The importance level also appears on the collapsed card header — so you can see your whole cast hierarchy at a glance without opening anything.
Characters — Detail
The Character Filter

At the top of the Characters section is a small All / Main cast toggle.

All shows everyone. Main cast hides any character rated ★★ or below — the background players, the minor characters, the one-scene appearances.

A note at the bottom tells you how many are hidden so you don't forget they exist. Switch back to All any time you need them.

💡 When you have 20 characters and you're looking for your protagonist, one click gets you there.
Section 2
Worlds

Worlds is for the settings of your story — and the most important field isn't geography or culture. It's Rules of This World.

What is different here from the real world? What are the limits? What can people do that we can't, or can't do that we can? Those answers go in the rules field.

You can have as many worlds as you need — a continent, a single building, a whole planet, a specific neighborhood. Each one gets its own card.

💡 The rules field is highlighted in the illustration because it's where the most interesting worldbuilding lives. Start there.
Section 3
Timeline

Timeline is for events — things that happened before your story, during it, or that you haven't placed in time yet.

You don't need real dates. "Three years before the story" works perfectly. So does "sometime after the war" or just a question mark.

Each entry has a when, a where, who was involved, what happened, and a notes field for the things you're still figuring out.

💡 Timelines have math. When you have a lot of entries, you start to notice when ages don't add up or events happen in the wrong order. That's useful information.
Section 4
Free Write

Free Write is open space. You create as many labeled areas as you want and write in them with zero structure.

Scene fragments, questions you can't answer, dialogue you overheard somewhere, images that keep coming back. Whatever doesn't fit in Characters or Worlds or Timeline — it goes here.

Name each space whatever helps you find it later. Change the name any time by clicking on it.

💡 Some of the best discoveries you'll make about your story will live here first, before you know where they belong.
Section 5
Writing Exercises

The Writing Gym has 33 exercises across five categories. None are graded. None have right answers.

They're prompts — ways of discovering things about your story you couldn't find by just describing it.

Notice the exercise uses Sera's name, not "Character A." The gym already knows who's in your story and uses them.

When you write something worth keeping, hit Save to Free Write and it lands there automatically.

💡 Hit 🎲 Surprise Me at any time for a completely random exercise from any category.
Exercises — Detail
The Dialogue Editor

Dialogue exercises use a special script-style editor. Type your characters' names at the top, then use the buttons to insert each character's name in the correct format.

The script area formats like a screenplay — character name in capitals, then what they say below.

It's designed to make writing dialogue fast — like you're watching a scene rather than describing one.

💡 After you've written a dialogue scene, check the Lessons section for a breakdown of how to turn that script into polished prose using dialogue tags.
Section 6
Lessons

The Lessons section has short craft lessons — things about technique rather than your specific story.

The first lesson is on dialogue tags — the phrase that tells the reader who's speaking. It shows five different ways to handle the same line of dialogue, with green examples that work and red examples that don't.

Every lesson ends with an exercise suggestion. The idea is: try the thing first, then read the lesson. It sticks better that way.

💡 More lessons are coming as the tool grows.
Section 7
Export & Backup

Click Export / Backup in the sidebar footer any time. You get three options:

Copy Text — puts everything on your clipboard. Paste straight into Google Docs.

Download Outline (.txt) — a formatted, readable summary of your whole project. Print it, share it, use it as a reference.

Download Backup (.json) — the full raw data. Load this back into Inkwell on any computer to restore your project.

💡 Every time you close the browser tab, a .json backup downloads automatically. Check your Downloads folder — it's probably already there.
The Promise
Saving

Inkwell saves automatically every time you click away from any field. You'll see a quiet ✓ Saved appear in the sidebar — blink and you'll miss it.

There is no Save button. There is nothing you need to remember to do. It just happens.

And every time you close the tab, a complete backup of your project downloads to your computer — dated and named, sitting in your Downloads folder, always there if you need it.

💡 If you ever need to restore: open the file, click "Load from Backup" at the bottom of the sidebar, find the most recent .json file. Everything comes back.
